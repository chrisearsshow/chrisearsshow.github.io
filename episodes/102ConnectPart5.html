#102. Connect Part 5.

Chris: Chapter 6 is about pinches and crunches.

Other Voice: Like when you get your skin caught in the door or when you want a six-pack.

C: Or when the other person does something that bothers you. Like they make a joke at your expense.

OV: But I can take a joke. I’m a good sport. Plus, complaining about it seems rude.

C: Maybe. The concern is that pinches, which might be considered petty initially, can eventually fester to become a bigger problem. Then it’s a crunch. Usually, if you catch it early, then it’s less likely for the emotions to build up.

OV: So we should raise an issue once we notice it?

C: Right. Here’s where paying attention to your own emotions is important.

OV: What if the other person didn’t mean anything by it?

C: There’s a difference between the intent of one’s behavior and the impact of that same behavior. Most people, if they had the choice, would want to know if their behavior is causing a pinch for others, even if they didn’t mean any harm.

OV: What if raising an issue makes it worse?

C: It might. But waiting even longer will make it that much worse.

OV: So how about crunches? How do you resolve them?

C: The trouble with crunches is that feelings have built up so we’re likely to start developing a story about the other person. Then we start selectively collecting data to build our case and to support our view of that other person.

OV: You still haven’t talked about how to resolve pinches or crunches.

C: The next chapter will cover how to deliver feedback.

OV: What?!

C: Okay, here are a few quick pointers:

  • Stay on your side of the net, which means not assuming what the other’s intentions are.
  • Identify what you’re feeling, especially vulnerable emotions like hurt, rejection, and sadness.
  • Convey your intention behind the feedback.
  • Be specific in your feedback.

OV: Never mind. That’s too much to remember.

C: That’s not even all of it.

OV: Talking about misunderstanding someone else’s behavior, here’s a story:

A police officer came upon a terrible wreck where the driver and passenger had been killed. As he looked upon the wreckage, a little monkey came out of the brush and hopped around the crashed car.

The officer looked down at the monkey and said "I wish you could talk." The monkey looked up at the officer and shook his head up and down.

"You can understand what I'm saying?" asked the officer.

Again, the monkey shook his head up and down. "Well, did you see this?"

"Yes," motioned the monkey.

"What happened?" The monkey pretended to have a can in his hand and turned it up by his mouth.

"They were drinking?" asked the officer.

"Yes."

"What else?"

The monkey pinched his fingers together and held them to his mouth.

"They were smoking marijuana?"

"Yes."

"What else?"

The monkey motioned "Screwing."

"They were screwing, too?" asked the astounded officer.

"Yes."

"Now wait, you're saying your owners were drinking, smoking, and screwing before they wrecked."

"Yes."

"What were you doing during all this?"

"Driving" motioned the monkey.
